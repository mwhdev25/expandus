Two very different philosophies. Notion is collaborative, cloud-first, and endlessly flexible. Obsidian is personal, offline-first, and built for deep knowledge work. Understanding the difference will save you months of switching.

## 📖 What Is Notion?

Notion is a cloud-based, block-editor workspace that combines notes, databases, wikis, and project management in a single product. It's the central hub for thousands of startups and remote teams.

**Notion is best for:**
- Team collaboration and shared wikis
- Project management alongside notes
- Building CRMs, content calendars, and custom dashboards
- Non-technical users who want flexibility without complexity

---

## 💎 What Is Obsidian?

Obsidian is a local-first, Markdown-based note-taking app. Your notes are stored as plain `.md` files on your own device — you own them completely. Its graph view reveals how your ideas connect over time.

**Obsidian is best for:**
- Personal knowledge management (PKM)
- Writers, academics, and researchers
- Privacy-conscious users
- Power users who love plugins and customization
- Building a Zettelkasten or evergreen note system

---

## ✍️ Writing Experience

**Winner: Obsidian** (for writing purists)

Obsidian's editor is pure Markdown — fast, distraction-free, and keyboard-driven. There's something immensely satisfying about writing in Obsidian's minimal interface.

Notion's editor is richer and more visual — good for structured documents with mixed media (images, databases, callouts), but it can feel heavy for longform prose.

---

## 🤝 Collaboration

**Winner: Notion**

Notion is built from the ground up for teams. Real-time multiplayer editing, granular permissions, team spaces, and sharing links make it the obvious choice for any collaborative workflow.

Obsidian collaboration requires the paid **Obsidian Sync** ($8/mo) or external tools like Git or iCloud — functional, but not as seamless as Notion.

---

## 🔗 Linking & Knowledge Graph

**Winner: Obsidian**

Obsidian's bidirectional linking and graph view are genuinely unique. Seeing a visual map of how your notes connect is a profound experience — especially after months of consistent note-taking.

Notion has links between pages, but no graph view or equivalent contextual linking depth.

---

## 🗄️ Databases & Structure

**Winner: Notion**

Notion's relational databases with multiple views (Table, Board, Calendar, Gallery) have no equivalent in Obsidian without heavy plugin configuration. For structured, relational data, Notion is in a different league.

---

## 🔒 Privacy & Data Ownership

**Winner: Obsidian**

Obsidian stores everything as plain `.md` files on your local drive. You own your data completely — no vendor lock-in, no cloud dependency.

Notion is a cloud service. Your data lives on their servers. While Notion does offer data export, the exported format is not perfectly clean Markdown.

---

## 🔌 Plugin Ecosystem

**Winner: Obsidian** (for power users)

Obsidian's community plugin ecosystem is extraordinary — 1,500+ plugins for spaced repetition, task management, LaTeX, canvas views, AI integration, and more.

Notion's integrations work through Zapier, the public API, and native connections — more limited but easier for non-technical users.

---

## 🏆 Verdict

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Choose Notion if...">
    - You work with a team that needs shared access
    - You want to combine notes, databases, and project management
    - You're not bothered by cloud storage and vendor dependency
    - You want something polished and ready to use out of the box
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Choose Obsidian if...">
    - You prioritize personal knowledge management and deep thinking
    - You want to own your notes as plain Markdown files
    - You're a researcher, writer, or academic
    - You love customization and the plugin ecosystem
    - Privacy and offline access are important to you
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Use both (the popular choice)">
    Many power users run Obsidian for personal notes and thinking, and Notion for team wikis and project management. They serve genuinely different purposes and complement each other well.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
